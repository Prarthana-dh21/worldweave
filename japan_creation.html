In the beginning, there was only chaos — a shapeless, swirling sea of mist and darkness. From this formless void emerged the first gods of the Shinto pantheon. Among them were Izanagi (He Who Invites) and Izanami (She Who Invites), a divine brother-sister pair chosen by the heavenly deities to bring shape and structure to the Earth.

Given a sacred jewel-tipped spear known as Ame-no-Nuhoko, the two gods stood on the Floating Bridge of Heaven and stirred the waters below. As they pulled the spear up, droplets fell from its tip and solidified to form the first land — Onogoro Island, a sacred place where they descended to fulfill their divine duty.

There, Izanagi and Izanami built a heavenly pillar and a palace, and they circled the pillar in opposite directions to perform their union. In their first attempt, Izanami, the woman, spoke first, which resulted in the birth of deformed offspring. Realizing the ritual needed to be properly balanced, they tried again — this time with Izanagi speaking first — and the union was successful.

From their divine marriage, the islands of Japan were born one by one: Awaji, Shikoku, Oki, Kyushu, Iki, Tsushima, Sado, and finally Honshu, the largest and most central island. This divine birth gave Japan the title of “Shinkoku” — the Land of the Gods.

But their story did not end in joy. As Izanami gave birth to the fire god, Kagutsuchi, she was severely burned and died. Heartbroken, Izanagi traveled to the underworld (Yomi) to bring her back. But when he found her, she had become a decaying spirit. Horrified, he fled, sealing the entrance to Yomi with a massive boulder, thus separating the worlds of the living and the dead forever.

Izanagi, tainted by his journey to the underworld, bathed in a sacred river to purify himself. From the cleansing water, new deities emerged — most notably Amaterasu, the sun goddess, who would become the ancestor of the imperial family of Japan.

Cultural Significance

This myth from the Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE) lies at the heart of Japanese Shinto beliefs. It explains not only the geographic creation of the islands, but also the sacred origins of Japan’s people, emperors, and traditions. The story reflects deep themes of cosmic balance, purity, and loss, and continues to be honored in rituals and festivals across Japan.

Even today, many shrines and imperial ceremonies trace their spiritual lineage back to Izanagi and Izanami, the creators of the islands and the first sacred beings of the land.
