In the rich and ancient mythology of Japan, Amaterasu, the radiant goddess of the sun, is one of the most revered deities. She is a symbol of light, life, and harmony — and the divine ancestor of Japan’s imperial family. But there was once a time when even the brilliance of Amaterasu was hidden, and the world plunged into total darkness.

Amaterasu ruled the heavens, bringing light and warmth to the world. Her brother, Susanoo, the storm god, was wild and unruly. Though powerful, he was impulsive and jealous, and his actions often disrupted the celestial order. One day, after being banished from the heavens, Susanoo, in a fit of rage, trashed Amaterasu’s sacred weaving hall, destroying her maidens and defiling her divine space.

Heartbroken and enraged, Amaterasu withdrew into the Ame-no-Iwato, the Heavenly Rock Cave, sealing the entrance behind her. Without her light, the heavens and the Earth were swallowed by darkness and despair. Crops withered, spirits wandered lost, and evil crept into the world. The gods of the celestial realm were alarmed — without the sun, all life was fading.

To coax Amaterasu out, the gods devised a clever plan. They gathered outside the cave and began a celebration. The goddess Ame-no-Uzume, known for her joy and dance, turned over a wooden bucket and began to dance wildly and comically — stamping her feet, shaking her hips, and even tearing off her clothes. The other gods laughed loudly, creating an uproar that echoed through the heavens.

Curious about the commotion, Amaterasu peeked out from the cave. There, she saw her reflection in a polished mirror the gods had placed nearby. Mesmerized and intrigued, she stepped forward — and at that moment, the strong god Tajikarao pulled open the cave and sealed it behind her, ensuring she could never hide again.

Light returned to the world. Harmony was restored, and Amaterasu resumed her place in the heavens, shining upon the Earth with renewed strength.

Cultural Significance

This myth reflects key Shinto values: the balance between chaos and order, the importance of community and cooperation, and the power of joy, ritual, and art to heal and restore. Amaterasu represents life-giving energy, and her story teaches that even the brightest must sometimes retreat — but through patience and unity, light can be restored.

Amaterasu is still worshipped in Japan today, especially at the Ise Grand Shrine, one of the most sacred Shinto sites. She is also considered the mythological ancestor of Japan’s imperial line, linking her directly to national identity and tradition.
