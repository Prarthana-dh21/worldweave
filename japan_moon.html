Long ago in ancient Japan, an old bamboo cutter named Taketori no Okina lived a simple life in the countryside. One day, while walking through a grove of golden bamboo, he noticed a mysterious glow coming from a stalk. Curious, he cut it open — and to his amazement, inside was a tiny, radiant girl no bigger than his thumb. Moved by her beauty and otherworldly presence, he took her home and raised her with his wife as their own child. They named her Kaguya-hime — “Princess of the Radiant Night.”

From that moment on, fortune smiled upon the old couple. Every time Taketori cut a bamboo stalk, he found gold or precious stones inside. As Kaguya-hime grew, so did her beauty — so dazzling that news of her spread far and wide. Soon, noblemen from all corners of Japan came to seek her hand in marriage.

But Kaguya-hime was no ordinary girl. She set each suitor an impossible task: to retrieve legendary treasures — a stone begging bowl of the Buddha, a jeweled branch from the island of Hōrai, a robe made of fire-rat fur, and more. One by one, the suitors failed, revealing greed, deceit, or cowardice. Even the Emperor of Japan himself, captivated by her grace, confessed his love — but Kaguya-hime gently refused, her eyes always filled with sorrow.

As the seasons passed, she grew increasingly distant and melancholic, especially under the full moon. One night, she finally revealed the truth: she was not of this world, but from the Moon Kingdom. Her time on Earth had been a punishment, or perhaps a test, and soon the moon people would come to take her back.

On the night of the full moon, a celestial procession descended from the sky. The Emperor sent guards to stop them, but no earthly force could resist their will. Clad in a shimmering robe of feathers, Kaguya-hime left behind a letter and an elixir of immortality for the Emperor, then ascended into the night, her sorrowful eyes the last thing the bamboo cutter saw.

The Emperor, devastated, burned the letter and the elixir at the summit of the tallest mountain — now known as Mount Fuji. To this day, smoke is said to rise from its peak, a sign of the Emperor’s eternal longing for the Moon Princess.

Cultural Significance

The tale of Kaguya-hime originates from the Taketori Monogatari (The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter), believed to be the oldest surviving piece of Japanese prose fiction, written around the 10th century. It blends folklore, fantasy, and moral lessons about love, impermanence, and the human condition.

The story holds a cherished place in Japanese literature and pop culture, symbolizing the ethereal beauty and unreachable nature of the divine. Kaguya-hime remains an iconic figure, often reimagined in films, anime, and theater — her tale reminding us of the bittersweet nature of fleeting love and the mysteries that lie beyond the stars.
